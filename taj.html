What Factors Explain the Decrease in Crime Since the 1990s?

In 1992, the United States experienced the highest crime rate, both property crime and violent crime, that the nation had ever seen. As the crime rate dropped, and continues to stay low, everyone and every thing under the sun has been given credit. From the 1994 Crime Act, to the mayor of every major city, to mandatory minimum sentancing, everyone thinks their policies are what caused the drop. As is usually the case with complex social phenomenon, it's complicated.

Violent and Property Crime Since the Late 1970's

The people who claimed the hiring more police officers and locking folks up would work appear to have a claim. As you can see from the chart above, the total number of sworn law enforcement officers began to climb in the late 1980s, along with the crime rate. The rate of imprisonment also climbed. In the early 1990s, it looks like we reached a turning point. Both violent and property crime began to decrease. As crime dropped, police hirings and prison rates continued to rise. In the early part of the 21st century, prison rates leveled off and began to drop slightly as the recession made it too difficult for States in particular to continue to house the large population of prisoners. California is a notable example of changing policies designed to release prisoners and reduce costs. The number of police officers continus to rise, possible as a result of homeland security type policies post 9-11.

No More Hotwires!

Another factor that influenced property crime rates was the development of new technologies that made it harder to steal cars. Around the time the crime wave peaked, car manufacturer's started including starter kill and keyless entry devices to new cars, making it harder to steal them. By 1998, the Honda Accord, historical the most hotwired vehicle in America, came with starter kill as a standard accessory. By 2002, European cars were required to be equiped with starter kill, and while this was not the case in the United States, most new cars are sold with it, greatly impacting motor vehicle theft rates.

Cell Phones and Muggings

A final piece of pervasive technology that impacted property crimes was the presence of cell phones. While it is the case that cell phones themselves can be stolen, the fact that people on the street can take photographs of crimes taking place, or call the police at any time, impacted certain street crimes. By 2012, more people owned smart phones than regular phones, and more than 80% of people owned some kind of cell phone. The additional fact that many smart phones now come with tracking software may also reduce larceny.
